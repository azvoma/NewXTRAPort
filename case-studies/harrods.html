Harrods Pre-Order Feature

Designed a luxury-grade pre-order experience that allowed high-intent customers to reserve coveted products before official release — eliminating the frustration of missed launches, reducing revenue support burden by 85%, and delivering a purchase flow that felt as premium as the brand itself.

A missing capability costing loyalty and revenue

Harrods had no pre-order capability. For limited-edition and high-demand products — where exclusivity and guaranteed access are core to the luxury proposition — this was a significant gap. High-intent customers were repeatedly missing launches, leading to frustration, repeated availability-checking, and missed conversion.

I missed out on the limited-edition bag I wanted because I didn't know when it would be available. By the time I heard about it, everything was sold out. There should be a way to reserve items in advance.

— Emma Thompson, Luxury Shopper

The design challenge was not just adding a pre-order button. It was making the entire experience feel effortless and exclusive — a luxury service, not an ecommerce transaction.

Research & Strategy

01 — Mixed-method research across customer segments

I used qualitative interviews with Harrods customers across segments, mapped limited-edition purchase journeys using analytics to identify friction and drop-off moments, and conducted competitive analysis of luxury pre-order patterns. The consistent finding across every method: customers valued guaranteed access and transparency above all other features.

02 — Persona-anchored design — the high-value luxury shopper

The primary persona was a time-poor, high-intent luxury shopper who expected exclusivity and service — not deals and friction. Every interaction was evaluated through one lens: does this feel like a luxury service, or an ecommerce transaction?

Design

03 — User flow and ideation

I designed end-to-end flows ensuring the pre-order journey integrated seamlessly with Harrods' existing shopping experience — familiar, predictable, and free of disruptive standalone processes. Crazy 8s explored different reservation models; a value vs effort matrix prioritised concepts that enhanced exclusivity without adding cognitive load.

04 — Mobile-first, WCAG 2.2 AA compliant execution

Low-fidelity wireframes validated terminology, information hierarchy, and accessibility before any visual polish was applied. High-fidelity screens were then built with proper colour contrast ratios, ARIA labels, semantic HTML structure, and responsive layouts.
