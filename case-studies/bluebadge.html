The Problem

The paper trail was failing the people who needed help most

The Blue Badge scheme provides parking concessions to around 2.5 million disabled people in England — yet the existing digital application service was making it harder, not easier, for eligible people to apply. The process was paper-heavy, confusing, and riddled with accessibility failures. Many applicants were giving up mid-way through, calling council support lines for help, or abandoning the process entirely.

The challenge was not just to digitise the service — it was to fundamentally redesign it following GDS standards and the GOV.UK Design System, building an experience that was genuinely usable by people with physical disabilities, visual impairments, and cognitive difficulties.

"I tried to apply online but it kept asking me questions I didn't understand. I gave up after 20 minutes and called the council instead. They told me I'd have to start again."

— Blue Badge applicant, discovery research participant
  • High drop-off rates — eligible people abandoning the process mid-application
  • Medical evidence requirements were unclear — applicants didn't know what documents were acceptable
  • Session timeouts wiped progress with no recovery mechanism
  • Screen reader and keyboard navigation failures excluding users with disabilities
  • Inconsistent status updates driving repeat calls to already-stretched council support teams
  • No auto-save — any interruption meant starting from scratch
My Role

Leading design end-to-end in an agile, multidisciplinary team

As Lead Product Designer, I owned the design of the service from discovery through to handoff — working within an agile team including a Product Manager, Tech Lead, QA Engineer, Content Designer, and User Researcher. I applied GOV.UK Design System patterns and GDS service standards throughout, and led accessibility audits and assistive technology testing at every stage of delivery.

Accessibility was embedded from the very first sketch — not treated as a phase at the end. Every design decision was validated against WCAG 2.2 AA criteria. I collaborated closely with the development team to ensure accessible, responsive implementation using semantic HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

Research

Understanding the people behind the application

Before any design work began, I ran a structured discovery phase to understand the real failure modes in the existing service. This included reviewing existing analytics to identify where people dropped off, shadowing council phone support agents to capture the most common queries, and conducting contextual interviews with applicants across three eligibility categories.

I coordinated stakeholder alignment across council processing staff, DfT policy teams, and accessibility specialists — translating diverse requirements into a single, coherent design direction. I also conducted a full heuristic audit of the existing service, mapping every failure point against WCAG 2.2 AA criteria.

01
Users with visual impairments could not navigate forms without assistance — core WCAG failures on colour contrast, focus indicators, and screen reader labelling
02
Evidence requirements were completely unclear — applicants didn't know what supporting documents were acceptable, why they were needed, or how to upload them
03
Session timeouts lost all progress with no warning or recovery — the single most common reason for support calls and abandoned applications
Insights

Defining the design challenge

Problem Statement: How might we redesign the Blue Badge application so that eligible people — including those with physical, visual, and cognitive disabilities — can complete their application independently, with confidence, without needing to call for support?

Two primary personas anchored every design decision throughout the project:

Gerald Thomas, 72 — confident on paper, anxious online. Needs clear progress indicators, plain language, and to never lose his work. Has limited digital experience but is motivated to apply independently.

Sarah Chen, 28 — tech-savvy but frustrated by medical jargon and unclear document requirements. Applying on behalf of a dependent; needs to easily pause, return, and track progress across multiple sessions.

Design implication: Because the most vulnerable users had the least tolerance for errors, ambiguity, or lost progress, I deprioritised feature complexity in favour of progressive disclosure, explicit guidance at the point of need, and robust session management.

Ideation

GOV.UK Design System as the structural backbone

The GOV.UK Design System's question-per-page pattern was the structural foundation of the redesign — each screen presenting only what was needed, progressively revealing complexity only when necessary. I mapped the full service architecture across four eligibility paths before sketching any screens, ensuring the branching logic was watertight before any visual design work began.

The key decision: I chose the question-per-page pattern over a single long form, despite early pressure from stakeholders to reduce 'the number of clicks.' The research was unambiguous — single focused questions significantly reduce errors and cognitive load, particularly for users with limited digital confidence or who are applying during a stressful period. Every extra click is worth it if it means fewer abandoned applications. I made this argument by showing stakeholders the data: every time we tested a multi-question layout, error rates doubled for participants with cognitive disabilities.

1
Progressive eligibility checking
Eligibility automatically assessed before requesting supporting evidence — preventing wasted effort and frustration for ineligible applicants
2
Contextual evidence guidance
Inline help at the exact point of need — explaining which documents were acceptable, why they were required, and how to upload them, in plain English
3
Persistent auto-save
Progress saved automatically after each completed section — applicants return at any time without losing work or starting over
4
Clear application status tracking
Consistent, proactive updates so applicants always know where their application stands — dramatically reducing repeat calls to council support teams
5
WCAG 2.2 AA accessibility by design
Focus management, 4.5:1 colour contrast minimums, visible persistent labels, screen reader compatibility, and keyboard navigation — baked in from the first wireframe
6
Session timeout warnings
Visible warnings at 5 and 2 minutes before timeout — giving users time to save progress and preventing sudden loss of completed sections
Design & Validation

From GDS wireframes to accessible high-fidelity

Low-fidelity wireframes were tested early with real users — including participants using screen readers, switch access devices, and keyboard-only navigation — before any high-fidelity design work began. I used GOV.UK Design System components wherever they met user needs, introducing new patterns only where existing ones genuinely couldn't serve the user.

Every WCAG 2.2 AA criterion was evidenced during design — not retrospectively at QA. Key decisions: focus management on every page transition, error messages written in plain language identifying the specific field and exactly how to fix it, and session checkpoint patterns ensuring no progress was ever silently lost.

The final high-fidelity screens applied GOV.UK's visual language — black headers, green action buttons, clear typographic hierarchy — while extending the system where the application flow required it:

Accessibility evidence gathered
Tested with screen reader users (NVDA, JAWS, VoiceOver), keyboard-only navigation, and switch access devices. Every criterion documented with pass/fail evidence.
Iterations made from testing
Simplified medical terminology throughout, enhanced visual hierarchy for critical information, refined error messages based on cognitive load research, and added session timeout warnings after user testing revealed silent timeouts as the primary failure point.

Takeaways

What I learned

Accessibility is not a checklist — it's a design philosophy. Embedding WCAG 2.2 AA from the first sketch produced a genuinely better experience for everyone, not only users with disabilities. Good accessibility practice and good UX practice are the same thing.
GDS patterns exist for a reason. The GOV.UK Design System components were built on extensive user research with real government service users. Working within them — rather than around them — accelerated delivery and improved quality at the same time.
Simplicity is the hardest design challenge. Getting complex eligibility logic into a series of clear, single-question screens required more design thinking than any visual work. Every screen went through multiple rounds of content and hierarchy iteration before it felt genuinely simple.
Research with the right people reveals what testing with proxies misses. Testing with users who actually live with the conditions the scheme covers uncovered failure modes — particularly around session timeouts and error recovery — that no amount of internal review would have caught.
Stakeholder breadth requires communication discipline. Aligning council teams, DfT policy, accessibility specialists, and technical delivery required structured communication and clear design rationale at every stage — not just at handoff.
